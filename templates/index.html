ABOUT PROJECT

Problem Statement:

Coal classification, any of various ways in which coal is grouped. Most classifications are based on the results of chemical analyses and physical tests, but some are more empirical in nature. Coal classifications are important because they provide valuable information to commercial users (e.g., for power generation and coke manufacturing) and to researchers studying the origin of coal.Coal may also be classified in grades using subjective terms (e.g., “low-sulfur coal,” “high-ash coal”) with reference to their impurities for commercial purposes.

Solution:

In this project, we will be building a deep learning model that can classify four types of Coal.The most common classification is based on rank, referring to the degree of coalification that has occurred. The rank of a coal is determined primarily by the depth of burial and temperature to which the coal was subjected over time. A web application is integrated with the model, from where the user can upload a coal image like Anthracites, Bituminous, Lignite and Peat, and see the analyzed results on UserInterface.

WE CLASSIFY

Anthracite

Anthracites are black to steel gray and have a brilliant, almost metallic lustre. They can be polished and used for decorative purposes. Hard and brittle, anthracites break with conchoidal fracture into sharp fragments.It is rarely used for this purpose today because of its limited abundance and relatively high cost and the ready availability of other sources of energy (e.g., natural gas and electricity) for heating purposes.

Bituminous

Bituminous is dark brown to black in colour and commonly banded, or layered.It is also called soft coal, the most abundant form of coalIt contain a fairly small amount of sulfur and cake (or “agglomerate”) easily are the only coals suited for making metallurgical coke-a hard, spongelike substance of almost pure carbon important for smelting iron ore.

Lignite

Lignite, generally yellow to dark brown or rarely black coal that formed from peat at shallow depths and temperatures lower than 100 °C (212 °F). It is the first product of coalification.In many countries it is considered to be a brown coal.It contains about 60 to 70 percent carbon.Many lignite beds lie close to the surface and are of great thickness, sometimes greater than 30 m (about 100 feet)

Peat

Peat, spongy material formed by the partial decomposition of organic matter, primarily plant material, in wetlands such as swamps, muskegs, bogs, fens, and moors. The development is favoured by warm moist climatic conditions.The wetlands in which peat forms are known as peatlands. The peat formed and housed in these special ecosystems is the largest natural terrestrial carbon store, and it sequesters more carbon than all other vegetation types in the world combined.
